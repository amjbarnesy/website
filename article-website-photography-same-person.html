Why your website and photography should come from the same person

By Adam Barnes · 10 March 2026 · 4 min read

Most businesses treat their website and their photography as two separate commissions. They find a web designer. Then, at some point — sometimes weeks later, sometimes on the morning of a deadline — they find a photographer. The two people involved rarely speak to each other. They work from different briefs, or no brief at all, and what arrives at the end is assembled rather than designed. You can feel it when you visit a site like that. Everything is technically there, but nothing quite fits.

The brief that arrives in two halves

The web designer gets told what the site needs to do and what it needs to say. The photographer gets told to come in and "get some good shots of the team and the space." Neither has seen the other's brief. The designer is working with a layout that assumes certain proportions, certain subjects, a certain amount of breathing room in the images. The photographer has no idea any of that exists. They shoot what they see, which is perfectly reasonable — it's just not what the site needs.

The result arrives as a folder of images that are technically well-exposed but structurally wrong. Too many verticals when the hero needs a horizontal. No negative space on the right because the photographer didn't know text was going to sit there. A team portrait cropped square when the layout wants a cinematic wide. The designer adapts as best they can. Images get stretched, reframed, pushed behind colour overlays to hide the awkward parts. The site gets built. It works. But something is missing.

It shows in the details

Some of the disconnects are obvious. A warm, earthy brand palette sitting next to photography that was lit in a cold studio against a grey backdrop. A hospitality business that feels genuinely welcoming in person, represented online by shots that could belong to any company in any town in the country. These aren't failures of skill — the photographer knew their craft, the designer knew theirs. They just never had a conversation about what the whole thing was supposed to feel like.

Other disconnects are subtler. The crop on a portrait works fine in isolation but looks cramped inside the card component it was built for. A building exterior is shot in flat, even light that makes it look like a stock photograph rather than somewhere you'd actually want to visit. The colours in the images don't reinforce the brand colours — they don't fight them either, they just coexist, a faint visual noise underneath everything else. None of it is wrong enough to point at. All of it adds up to a site that doesn't quite hold together.

When one person does both

When the same person designs the site and takes the photographs, the brief only needs to be written once — and the decisions that would otherwise require a long email chain between two freelancers happen in minutes, sometimes before the shoot has even started. The photography is composed with the layout in mind: negative space in the right places, portrait crops that work for both a square thumbnail and a full-bleed hero, colours that sit naturally inside the palette rather than fighting it.

The website, in turn, gets designed around photography that actually exists — not around optimistic placeholders that look nothing like what eventually arrives. If I know I'm shooting a particular location, I know what I'll come back with. I can design around it. The images and the layout grow from the same set of decisions, and you can see it in the result. The site looks like it was made for those photographs, because it was.

A real example

I worked with a hospitality business — a venue that wanted to attract private hire bookings, corporate events and weekend visitors all from the same site. Before the shoot day I had a rough wireframe of the site structure: what each section needed, which spaces needed to be shown and in what way, where a landscape would work and where a portrait would be more useful. I arrived with a shot list built around the website rather than around a general sense of "let's get the place looking good." Each section of the finished site had the image it needed before a single line of code was written. The photography informed the layout and the layout had already informed the photography. Neither one was an afterthought.

The hidden cost of splitting it

Two separate commissions feel cheaper until you add everything up. Two briefs. Two sets of revision rounds. Two people billing for their time, neither of whom can answer questions about the other's work when something doesn't fit. Then the integration pass at the end: resizing files, asking the photographer if they have an alternative crop, going back and forth over whether the lighting on a particular shot clashes with the hero section. That work takes time and costs money, and it doesn't appear on any quote because nobody factored it in.

There is also a less visible cost. Every compromise made during integration — every image pushed behind an overlay, every crop that almost works — is a small reduction in the quality of the final site. Those reductions accumulate. The site that gets launched is not quite the site that was designed, and the photography is not quite the photography that was taken. Both have been adapted to suit a situation neither was built for.

"The images and the layout grow from the same set of decisions — and you can see it in the result."

This is why I offer web design and photography as a combined service. Not because it's a convenient package to sell, but because the work is genuinely better when one person can see the whole picture from the start. If you're thinking about a new website and you need photography to go with it, it's worth considering whether the person you hire for one should be the same person you hire for the other.
